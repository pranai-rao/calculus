A Short Review of Polar Functions

Recall that a polar coordinate system is a coordinate system based on the idea that the location of a point might be best described by its distance from a fixed point and its angular direction.

This system in a plane consists of a fixed point \(O\), called the pole and a ray emanating from the pole, called the polar axis.

Just as with a cartesian (rectangular) plane, where a grid is placed over the axis, a polar grid is placed over the polar axis.

Converting Between Polar and Cartesian Coordinates

There are some simple relationships to convert between coordinates:

\(\begin{align}&x =r\cos(\theta) \\ &r^2 =x^2+y^2\end{align}\)  \(\begin{align}&y =r\sin(\theta) \\ &\tan(\theta)=\frac{y}{x}\end{align}\)

Lines and Circles

Lines Circles
\(\theta=k\) Centered at the origin:
\(r=k\)
Centered on the x-axis:
\(r=k\cos(\theta)\)
Centered on the y-axis:
\(r=k\sin(\theta)\)

Points of Intersection

The other vital piece of information is to be able to determine how many times and where two (or more) polar curves intersect.

Just like in cartesian coordinates, where we needed ot find a point with the same \(x\) and \(y\) value, we will need to find a point with the same \(r\) and \(\theta\) value. Note: Graphs can be deceiving!

Example

Find the points of intersection of \(r=\sin(\theta)\) and \(r=1-\sin(\theta)\).

$$\begin{align}\sin(\theta) &= 1-\sin(\theta) \\ 2\sin(\theta) &= 1 \\ \sin(\theta) &= \frac{1}{2}\end{align}$$

\(\theta_\mathrm{I}=\frac{\pi}{6}\)  \(\theta_\mathrm{II}=\frac{5\pi}{6}\)

\((\frac{1}{2},\frac{\pi}{6})\)  \((\frac{1}{2},\frac{5\pi}{6})\)
