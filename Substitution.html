Substitution Method

CAS programs (and the TI-89) can evaluate extremely complicated integrals. Prior to CAS, tables were used.

Transformation methods are methods for converting unfamiliar integrals into familiar integrals.

For example: $$\int f(x)dx=F(x)+C$$ $$\int x^3dx=\frac{1}{4}x^4+C$$

But problems like \(\int xe^{x^2}dx\) and \(\int\sin(x)\cos(x)dx\) or \(\int\frac{2x+3}{x^2-3x+4}dx\) or \(\int x^2\sin(x)dx\) cannot be solved with previously used methods.

Substitution Method

Motivated by the chain rule from the viewpoint of antidifferentiation, let \(F\) be an antiderivative of \(f\) and let \(g\) be a differentiable function.

Chain rule: $$\begin{align}\frac{d}{dx}[F(g(x))] &= F'(g(x))g'(x) \\ \int F'(g(x))g'(x)dx &= F(g(x))+C \\ \int f(g(x))g'(x)dx &= F(g(x))+C\end{align}$$

Let \(u=g(x)\). Then \(\frac{du}{dx}=g'(x)\). If we write this in differential form, \(du=g'(x)dx\).

If you rewrite the indefinite integral with \(u\): $$\int f(u)du=F(u)+C$$

The focus is not on the interpretation of \(du=g'(x)\); this is just useful book-keeping

Example

  1. Evaluate \(\int(x^2+1)^{50}\)

    2. \(u=x^2+1\)

    \(du=2xdx\)

    $$\int u^{50}du$$ $$\frac{1}{51}u^{51}+C$$ $$\frac{1}{51}(x^2+1)^{51}+C$$

Guidelines for Substitution

  1. Look for a product of a composition of functions \(f(g(x))\) along with a derivative of the inner function.
    • Rewrite by substituting
    • Set \(u=g(x)\) and \(du=g'(x)dx\)
  2. If successful, then try to evaluate the resulting integration in terms of \(u\).
  3. If successful, then replace \(u\) with \(g(x)\).

Examples

  1. Evaluate \(\int\sin(5x-2)dx\)

    2. \(u=5x-2\)

    \(du=5dx\to dx=\frac{1}{5}du\)

    $$\int\sin(u)\cdot\frac{1}{5}du$$ $$-\frac{1}{5}\cos(u)+C$$ $$-\frac{1}{5}\cos(5x-2)+C$$

  2. Evaluate \(\int\frac{1}{(\frac{1}{3}x-8)^5}dx\)

    3. \(u=\frac{1}{3}x-8\)

    \(du=\frac{1}{3}dx \to dx=3du\)

    $$\int\frac{1}{u^5}\cdot 3du$$

    $$\int3u^{-5}du$$

    $$-\frac{3}{4}u^{-4}+C$$

    $$-\frac{3}{4}(\frac{1}{3}x-8)^{-4}+C$$
