There are three common choices for x-values to determine rectangle height: left and right endpoints, and midpoints of subintervals.

What Rectangle Method Should be Used?

  • Left endpoint rectangles tend to under/overestimate based on the curve
  • Right endpoint rectangles also tend to under/overestimate based on the curve
  • Midpoint rectangles tend to make a better estimate than RRAM and LRAM

Formulas

\(L_n=\Delta x(f(x_1^*)+f(x_2^*)+...+f(x_{n-1}^*)+f(x_n^*))\)

\(R_n=\Delta x(f(x_1^*)+f(x_2^*)+...+f(x_{n-1}^*)+f(x_n^*))\)

\(M_n=\Delta x(f(x_1^*)+f(x_2^*)+...+f(x_{n-1}^*)+f(x_n^*))\)

Trapezoidal Rule

MRAM often approximates the area under the curve better than LRAM or RRAM, but trapezoids can do it better.

Trapezoidal Approximation Example

Area of a trapezoid: \(A=\frac{1}{2}(b_1+b_2)\)

Area under a curve:

$$A=\frac{1}{2}h[(y_0+y_1)+(y_1+y_2)+...+(y_{n-1}+y_n)]$$

Like RAM, we will make the height "h" constant across subintervals. So \(h=\frac{b-a}{n}\) where \(n\) is the number of subintervals.

$$\begin{aligned} A &=\frac{1}{2}h(y_0+y_1+y_1+y_2+y_2+y_3+...+y_{n-1}+y_n) \\ &=\frac{1}{2}h(y_0+2y_1+2y_2+...+2y_{n-1}+y_n) \end{aligned}$$

If the function \(f\) is continuous on \([a, b]\) and if \(f(x)\geq0\) for all \(x\) in \([a, b]\), then the area \(A\) under the curve \(y=f(x)\) over the interval \([a,b]\) can be approximated by: $$A\approx T_n=\frac{1}{2}(\frac{b-a}{n})(y_0+2y_1+...+2y_{n-1}+y_n)$$

Simpson's Rule

So far, all approximation methods used lines to approximate the area under a curve. Simpson's Rule uses curves to approximate the area under the curve; specifically, quadratic curves.

Area under a parabolic arc from a to b: $$A\approx\frac{1}{3}\Delta x(y_0+4y_1+y_2)$$

With more than one parabola: $$\begin{aligned}A &\approx\frac{1}{3}\Delta x(y_0+4y_1+y_2+y_2+4y_2+y_4) \\ &\approx\frac{1}{3}\Delta x(y_0+4y_1+2y_2+4y_3+y_4)\end{aligned}$$

Note: Coefficients of 2 and 4 alternate.

If we continued, the first and last \(y\) would have a coefficient of 1. The \(y\) next to the \(y\) with a coefficient of 1 must have a coefficient of 4. In between the coefficient 4, the coefficient pattern alternates between 4 and 2, but must work out evenly.

There must be an even number of subintervals.

Error

With all these methods, there are two sources of error. First, intrinsic or turncation error (due to the approximation formula). Second, the roundoff error (introduced in calculations).

In general, increasing \(n\) decreases truncation error but increases roundoff error.

In practical applications, it is important to understand how large \(n\) must be to ensure that a specific degree of accuracy is obtained.

Roundoff error is beyond our scope, so we will focus on truncation error. Though the precise error will not be found, we will find the upper bound of error.

Midpoint and Trapezoidal Error Bounds

If \(f''\) is continuous on \([a,b]\) and if \(K_2\) is the maximum value of \(\mid f''(x)\mid\)

  1. \(\mid E_m\mid=\mid A-M_n\mid\leq\frac{K_2}{24n^2}\mid b-a\mid^3\)
  2. \(\mid E_T\mid=\mid A-T_n\mid\leq\frac{K_2}{12n^2}\mid b-a\mid^3\)
