An Overview of the Area Problem

Differential calculus is about the slope problem.

The formula for the area of polygons have been well known, but curved shapes, for instance circles, have always been difficult to find.

Archimedes determined that the area of regions bounded by circular arcs (e.g., parabolas, arcs, and curves) can be found by a process later called the method of exhaustion.

Method of Exhaustion

When applied to circles, a succession of regular polygons are inscribed in the circle, allowing the number of sides to increase indefinitely.

As the number of sides increase, area inside the circle becomes exhausted. Accordingly, the area of the polygon becomes an increasingly better approximation of the circle.

\(A(n)\) represents the area of an n-sided polygon inscribed in a circle with a radius of 1. As n increases. \(A(n)\) resembles \(\pi\)

Thus, for a circle of \(r=1\): $$\lim_{n\to\infty}A(n)=\pi$$

Because Greek mathematicians did not trust the concept of infinity, the method of exhaustion was a very difficult process until Newton and Leibniz that a general idea using limits was created.

The Area Problem

Given a function \(f\) that is continuous and nonnegative on an interval \([a, b]\), find the area between the graph of \(f\) and the interval \([a, b]\) on the x-axis

The Rectangle Method

Applying Archimedes' method of exhaustion:

Divide the interval \([a, b]\) into \(n\) equal subintervals. In each subinterval construct a rectangle that extends from the x-axis to any point on the curve that is above the subinterval.

For each \(n\), the total area of the rectangles can be viewed as an approximation of the exact area under the curve over the interval \([a, b]\). As \(n\) increases, the approximation will improve.

The Antiderivative Method

Though the rectangle method is intuitive, limits that result can only be evaluated sometimes.

In the late 17th century, Newton and Leibniz independently discovered a relationship between area and derivatives.

If \(f\) is a nonnegative continuous function on \([a, b]\), and if \(A(x)\) denotes the area underneath the graph of \(f\) over the interval \([a,x]\), where \(x\) is any point in the interval \([a, b]\), then: $$A'(x)=f(x)$$

The antiderivative method is the more efficient method of computing the area under a curve, but it is the rectangle method that is used to formally define the notion of area, allowing us to prove mathematical results. The rectangle method can also be applied to a broader range of problems such as the volume of a solid, length of a curve, etc..


Notes:

Area of a rectangle: \(A=bh\)

Area of a triangle: \(A=\frac{1}{2}bh\)

Area of a trapezoid: \(A=\frac{1}{2}h(b_1+b_2)\)
