Integration by Parts, Part 1

With the substitution method, we were able to reverse the process of the chain rule.

IBP is an antideirvative formulation of the formula for differentiating a product of two functions. In other words, a general method for attacking the form: $$\int f(x)g(x)dx$$

Let \(G(x)\) be the derivative of \(g(x)\), where \(G'(x)=g(x)\). The product rule for differentiating \(f(x)G(x)\):

$$\begin{align}\frac{d}{dx}[f(x)G(x)] &= f'(x)G(x)+f(x)G'(x) \\ &= f'(x)G(x)+f(x)g(x) \end{align}$$

In integral form: \(\int[f(x)g(x)+f'(x)G(x)]dx=f(x)G(x)+C\)

Manipulating this integral using known properties:

$$\begin{align} \int[f(x)g(x)+f'(x)G(x)]dx &= f(x)G(x) \\ \int f(x)g(x)dx+\int f'(x)G(x)dx &= f(x)G(x) \\ \int f(x)g(x)dx &= f(x)G(x)-\int f'(x)G(x)dx\end{align}$$

This process is called integration by parts.

Integration by Parts

In practice, the IbP formula is written:

\(\begin{align}u &= f(x) \\ du &= G'(x)dx\\ \end{align}\) \(\begin{align}dv &= G'(x)dx \\ v &= \sin(x)\end{align}\)

Thus: $$\int udv=uv-\int vdu$$

Guidelines for IbP

  • Main goal: Choose \(u\) and \(dv\) to obtain an integral that is easier to find than the first.
  • A strategy that often works is to choose \(u\) and \(dv\) so that \(u\) becomes "simpler" when differentiated, while leaving a \(dv\) that can be readily integrated to obtain \(v\).

Example

Consider \(\int x\cos(x)dx\)

\(\begin{align}u &= x \\ du &= dx\end{align}\) \(\begin{align}dv &= -\sin(x)dx \\ v &= \sin(x)\end{align}\)

LIATE Strategy

Another useful strategy for choosing \(u\) and \(dv\) can be applied when the integrand is a product of two functions from different categories in the LIATE list.

LIATE = Logarithmic, Inverse, Algebraic, Trigonometric, Exponential

"LIATE" is a good way to remember the list. Though this does not work all the time, it works enough to be useful.

Examples

  1. \(\int x\cos(x)dx\)

    2. \(\begin{align}u &= x \\ du &= dx\end{align}\) \(\begin{align}dv &= -\cos(x)dx \\ v &= \sin(x)\end{align}\)

    $$\begin{align}\int udv &= uv-\int vdu \\ \int x\cos(x)dx &= x\sin(x)-\int-\sin(x)dx \\ &= x\sin(x)+\cos(x) + C\end{align}$$

  2. \(\int xe^xdx\)

    3. \(\begin{align}u &= x \\ du &= dx\end{align}\) \(\begin{align}dv &= e^xdx \\ v &= e^x\end{align}\)

    $$\begin{align}\int udv &= uv-\int vdu \\ \int xe^xdx &= xe^x-\int e^xdx \\ &= xe^x-e^x+C\end{align}$$
