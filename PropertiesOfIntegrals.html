Properties of Integrals

Constant Multiple Rule

\(\int kf(x)dx=k\int f(x)dx=kF(x)+C\)

Sum and Difference Rule

\(\int[f(x)\pm g(x)]dx=\int f(x)dx\pm \int g(x)dx\)

Integral Curves

Graphs of the antiderivatives of \(f\) are called integral curves of \(f\). If \(y=F(x)\) is any integral curve of \(f(x)\) then all other integral curves are vertical translations of this curve since \(y=F(x)+C\). For example, \(y=\frac{1}{3}x^3\) is one integral curve for \(f(x)=x^2\), and all other curves take the form \(\frac{1}{3}x^3+C\).

Differential Equations

Suppose that \(f(x)\) is a known function and we are interested in finding a function \(F(x)\) such that \(y=F(x)\) staistfies the equation: \(\frac{dy}{dx}=f(x)\).

This is called a differential function because it involves a derivative of an unknown function, rather than a number. Recognize that solutions of this equation are the antiderivatives of \(f(x)\).

Slope Fields

\(\frac{dy}{dx}\) can be thought of as the slope of the tangent line to a curve. So at a point \((x, y)\) on an integral curve of the equation \(\frac{dy}{dx}=f(x)\), the slope of the tangent line is \(f(x)\).

We can find the slopes of the tangent lines to integral curves without actually knowing the function. For example, if \(\frac{dy}{dx}=\sqrt{x^2+1}\), then at \(x=1\) the slope of the tangent line is \(\sqrt{(1)^2+1}=\sqrt{2}\).

Geometric Description

One way to represent all the possible integral curves of a differential equation is by calculating the slopes of the tangent lines through points in a rectangular plane.
