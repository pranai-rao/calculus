Antidifferentiation

A function \(F\) is called an antiderivative of a function \(f\) on a given open interval if \(F'(x)=f(x)\) for all \(x\) in the interval.

For example, \(F(x)=\frac{1}{3}x^3\) is the antiderivative of \(f(x)=x^2\) on the interval \((-\infty, \infty)\).

Note that   \(F(x)=\frac{1}{3}x^3+7\),  \(F(x)=\frac{1}{3}x^3-2.4\),  and  \(F(x)=\frac{1}{3}x^3+\pi\)  are all antiderivatives of \(f(x)\).

Note that  \(F(x)=\frac{1}{3}x^3+x\) is NOT an antiderivative of \(f(x)\).

If \(F(x)\) is any antiderivative of \(f(x)\) on an open interval, then for any constant \(C\), the function \(F(x)+C\) is also an antiderivative on that interval. Moreover, each antiderivative of \(f(x)\) on the interval can be expressed in the form \(F(x)+C\) by choosing the constant \(C\) appropriately.

The Indefinite Integral

The process of finding antiderivatives is called antidifferentiation or integration.

Thus, if \(\frac{d}{dx}F(x)=f(x)\), then integrating \(f(x)\) produces the form of \(F(x)+C\).

To emphasize this process, we rewrite the relationship using integral notation: $$\int f(x)dx=F(x)+C$$

Notation: \(\int\) is the integral sign, \(f(x)\) is the integrand, \(dx\) is the differential (i.e., variable of integration), \(F(x)\) is the antiderivative, and \(C\) is the constant.

\(\int x^2dx=\frac{1}{3}x^3+C\) is equivalent to \(\frac{d}{dx}[\frac{1}{3}x^3]=x^2\)

One way to think about it is that integration and derivatives are inverse operations: \(\frac{d}{dx}[]\) and \(\int[]dx\)

The symbol "\(dx\)" serves to represent the independent variable. If the variable is \(t\), it should be \(\frac{d}{dt}\) and \(dt\) respectively.


Examples:

Derivative Formula Equivalent Integration Formula
\(\frac{d}{dx}[x^3]=3x^2\) \(\int [3x^2]dx=x^3+C\)
\(\frac{d}{dx}[\sqrt{x}]=-\frac{1}{2}x^{-\frac{1}{2}}\) \(\int[-\frac{1}{2}x^{-\frac{1}{2}}]dx=\sqrt{x}+C\)
\(\frac{d}{d\theta}[\tan(\theta)]=\sec^2(\theta)\) \(\int[\sec^2(\theta)]d\theta=\tan(\theta)+C\)
\(\frac{d}{dt}[t^{\frac{3}{2}}]=\frac{3}{2}t^{\frac{1}{2}}\) \(\int[\frac{3}{2}t^{\frac{1}{2}}]dt=t^{\frac{3}{2}}+C\)

Finding Antiderivatives

Integration is educated guesswork. For example: $$\frac{d}{dx}\sin(x)=\cos(x)\therefore \int\sin(x)dx=\sin(x)+C$$

Watch for negatives! $$\frac{d}{dx}\cos(x)=-\sin(x)$$$$\int\sin(x)dx=-\cos(x)+C$$
