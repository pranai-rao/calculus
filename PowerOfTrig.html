Powers of Trigonometric Functions

Using Trigonometric Identities

Up until now, we have been able to take the antiderivatives of basic trigonometric functions. Now, we will look at powers of trigonometric functions. The two most important functions we will look at are \(\int\sin^2(x)dx\) and \(\int\cos^2(x)dx\).

Unlike \(\csc^2(x)\) or \(\sec^2(x)\), these are not simple antiderivatives to take.

Recall that \(\cos(2x)\) has three trigonometric identities. Since \(\cos(2x)=\cos^2(x)-\sin^2(x)\) and \(\sin^2(x)+\cos^2(x)=1\):

$$\begin{align}\cos(2x) &= \cos^2(x)-\sin^2(x) \\ &= 1-2\sin^2(x) \\ &= 2\cos^2(x)-1\end{align}$$

Using the identities of \(\cos(2x)\) containing only \(\sin^2(x)\) or only \(\cos^2(x)\), we can integrate the original problems.

If \(\cos(2x)=1-2\sin^2(x)\), then \(\sin^2(x)=\frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{2}\cos(2x)\).

$$\int\sin^2(x)dx$$

$$\int\frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{2}\cos(2x)dx$$

$$\frac{1}{2}x-\frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{2}\sin(2x)+C$$

$$\frac{1}{2}x-\frac{1}{4}\sin(2x)+C$$

Since \(\sin(2x)=2\sin(x)\cos(x)\):

$$\frac{1}{2}x-\frac{1}{2}\sin(x)\cos(x)+C$$

But what about higher powers?

It is, in fact, possible to apply the same rules to trigonometric functions of higher powers.

$$\int\sin^4(x)dx$$

$$\int\sin^3(x)\cdot\sin(x)dx$$

\(\begin{align}u &= \sin^3(x)dx \\ du &= 3\sin^2(x)\cos(x)dx\end{align}\) \(\begin{align}dv &= \sin(x)dx \\ v &= -\cos(x)\end{align}\)

$$\begin{align}\int\sin^3(x)\sin(x)dx &= -\sin^3(x)\cos(x) + \int3\sin^2(x)\cos^2(x)dx \\ \end{align}$$

At this point, the integral could be rewritten in terms of \(\sin^2(x)\) or \(\cos^2(x)\) and solved. However, this is a long process. Instead, mathematicians have created formulas, reduction formulas, that allow us to simplify the process.

Reduction Formulas

$$\int\sin^n(x)dx=-\frac{1}{n}\sin^{n-1}(x)\cos(x)+\frac{n-1}{n}\int\sin^{n-2}(x)dx$$

$$\int\cos^n(x)dx=\frac{1}{n}\cos^{n-1}(x)\sin(x)+\frac{n-1}{n}\int\cos^{n-2}(x)dx$$

$$\int\tan^n(x)dx=\frac{1}{n-1}\tan^{n-1}(x)-\int\tan^{n-2}(x)dx$$

$$\int\cot^n(x)dx=-\frac{1}{n-1}\cot^{n-1}(x)-\int\cot^{n-2}(x)dx$$

$$\int\sec^n(x)dx=\frac{1}{n-1}\sec^{n-2}(x)\tan(x)+\frac{n-2}{n-1}\int\sec^{n-2}(x)dx$$

$$\int\csc^n(x)dx=-\frac{1}{n-1}\csc^{n-2}(x)\cot(x)+\frac{n-2}{n-1}\int\csc^{n-2}(x)dx$$

Example

  1. Integrate \(\int\cos^4(x)dx\)

    2. $$\begin{align}\int\cos^n(x)dx &= \frac{1}{n}\cos^{n-1}(x)\sin(x)+\frac{n-1}{n}\int\cos^{n-2}(x)dx \\ \int\cos^4(x)dx &= \frac{1}{4}\cos^3(x)\sin(x)+\frac{3}{4}\int\cos^2(x)dx \\ &= \frac{1}{4}\cos^3(x)\sin(x)+\frac{3}{4}\int\frac{1}{2}+\frac{1}{2}\cos(2x)dx \\ &= \frac{1}{4}\cos^3(x)\sin(x)+\frac{3}{4}\bigg{[}\frac{1}{2}x+\frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{2}\sin(2x)\bigg{]}+C \\ &= \frac{1}{4}\cos^3(x)\sin(x)+\frac{3}{8}x+\frac{3}{8}\sin(x)\cos(x)+C\end{align}$$

Integrating Products of Sines and Cosines

\(\int\sin^m(x)\cos^n(x)dx\) Procedure Relevant Identities
If \(n\) is odd
  • Split off a factor of \(\cos(x)\)
  • Apply the relevant identity
  • Make the substitution \(u=\cos(x)\)
 \(\cos^2(x)=1-\sin^2(x)\)
If \(m\) is odd
  • Split off a factor of \(\sin(x)\)
  • Apply the relevant identity
  • Make the substitution \(u=\cos(x)\)
 \(\sin^2(x)=1-\cos^2(x)\)
If \(m\) and \(n\) are even
  • Use the relevant identities to reduce the powers on \(\sin(x)\) and \(\cos(x)\)
 \(\begin{cases} \sin^2(x)=\frac{1}{2}(1-\cos(2x)) \\ \cos^2(x)=\frac{1}{2}(1+\cos(2x)) \end{cases}\)

Example

  1. Integrate \(\int\sin^4(x)\cos^5(x)dx\)

    2. $$\int\sin^4(x)\cos^5(x)dx$$

    $$\int\sin^4(x)\cos^4(x)\cos(x)dx$$

    $$\int\sin^4(x)(1-\sin^2(x))^2\cos(x)dx$$

    \(\begin{align} u &= \sin(x) \\ du &= \cos(x)dx \end{align}\)

    $$\int u^4(1-u^2)^2du$$

    $$\int u^4(1-2u^2+u^4)du$$

    $$\int u^4-2u^6+u^8du$$

    $$\frac{1}{5}u^5-\frac{2}{7}u^7+\frac{1}{9}u^9+C$$

    $$\frac{1}{5}\sin^5(x)-\frac{2}{7}\sin^7+\frac{1}{9}\sin^9+C$$

Integrating Products of Tangents and Secants

\(\int\tan^m(x)\sec^n(x)dx\) Procedure Relevant Identities
If \(n\) is even
  • Split off a factor of \(\sec^2(x)\)
  • Apply the relevant indentity
  • Make the substitution \(u=\tan(x)\)
 \(\sec^2(x)=1+\tan^2(x)\)
If \(m\) is odd
  • Split off a factor of \(\sec(x)\tan(x)\)
  • Apply the relevant identity
  • Make the substitution \(u=\sec(x)\)
 \(\tan^2(x)=\sec^2(x)-1\)
If \(m\) is even and \(n\) is odd
  • Use the relevant identities to reduce the integrand to powers of \(\sec(x)\) alone
  • Then use the reduction formula for powers of \(\sec(x)\)
 \(\tan^2(x)=\sec^2(x)-1\)

Example

  1. Integrate \(\int\tan^3(x)\sec^3(x)dx\)

    2. $$\int\tan^3(x)\sec^3(x)dx$$

    $$\int\tan^2(x)\sec^2(x)\tan(x)\sec(x)dx$$

    $$\int(\sec^2(x)-1)\sec^2(x)\tan(x)\sec(x)dx$$

    \(\begin{align} u &= \sec(x) \\ du &= \sec(x)\tan(x) \end{align}\)

    $$\int(u^2-1)u^2du$$

    $$\int u^4-u^2du$$

    $$\frac{1}{5}u^5-\frac{1}{3}u^3+C$$

    $$\frac{1}{5}\sec^5(x)-\frac{1}{3}\sec^3(x)+C$$
